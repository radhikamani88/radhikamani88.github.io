Game overview

To help understand the rest of the document, I will explain the necessary portions of the game first.

Set in Antartica, a virus in Antartic science station has infected a patient. The goal is to find patient 0 before everyone is infected, using clues and tools provided by the game app and social communication.

At any point in the game, there are 2 groups of people: Researchers and Infected. During the game, we get to test biological samples of other researchers and check if they are infected

Approach

I suggested that we divide 12 weeks into 6 sprints of 2 weeks each as that minimizes administrative overhead and allows us to track progress effectively. We did the following steps in the 6 sprints and each week we would produce a report documenting our progress, findings and results in that sprint.

Research

Competitive analysis — does game concept need redesign?

As none of us had worked in this domain before and had not studied similar social deduction games we decide to first spend time doing that. Mafia, The resistance and Avalon were the 3 games evaluated by us. After each game, team members provided feedback, listing one thing they enjoyed and one thing they wanted improved.

We found out that the competitors had the following drawbacks. However, the game mechanics of Latitude 90 was trying to address these issues and hence we decided to retain those aspects of game play of Latitude 90.

Goal Competitors Latitude 90 - Origin
Player involvement
In these games, players usually die midway game due to no fault of theirs. This reduced game engagement. Players don't die in Latitude 90 however and hence we found it to be superior in that respect.
Help players make conclusive informed decisions
As there was no computer involved in these games, it was very hard for players to find any conclusive evidence to make progress in finding who the adversary was. This usually led to dissapointment as players were killed incorrectly and forced to sit out the rest of the game.

no, overall, our conclusion was that the game concept and mechanics are well designed and did not see any need to improve on those improve on them based on competitive analysis.

Playtesting — Ethnographic study

As none of us were experts in social deduction games, we decided to conduct ethnographic studies to study the behavior of players when they are playing this game. This was viable since we were in an university and it was easy to find participants who were interested in social deduction games and had played such games before.

4 of 5 total sessions were with the same two playtest groups. It was this iterative user feedback that powered our Agile process. We collected 100+ responses and 10+ hours of footage. Playtesting was ideal as it let us both observe users naturally and receive direct feedback afterwards. During each game, the team recorded notable interactions in-game (positive and negative). After each game, we conducted moderated sessions where each participant provided feedback. We had the following findings:

Social interactions are heart and soul
Players were so engrossed in discussion, deception, deduction, discovery and betrayal that they forget their phones. Players enjoy the process of discussion, deduction, deception, discovery, and betrayal. These interactions had such a lasting impact that they often carried over multiple rounds of the game.
UI confusing
We found that the initial version of the UI that we were testing had several UI issues. Newcomers found it hard to navigate the app. Experienced users were confused by the game and accidentaly misled to reveal game-breaking information during the game due to the app.
Unclear how the app fit with the rest of the game
While the app was providing useful information and clues and people liked the app, people were not clear about what the role of the app is. There weren't clear what steps of the game are played in the app vs. in the real world.

7 independent heuristic evaluations

The purpose of this research was to catch any issues we may have potentially missed in playtesting and identify how we would be able to address them as we made our transition from web to mobile. We found 2 primary issues:

No system feedback
Actions did not offer confirmation. In end-game states game outcome was decided without explicitly informing every user
No error prevention
Unintentional errors were common and public and private notifications were indistinguishable

Final design

Design goals

Improve feature discoverability
Discover casting vote, mark suspicion etc. easily. Provide good visual cues for events occurred and available actions
Always inform system state
Enable them to make most informed decisions
Don't try to replace the real world
Augment social interactions with mobile game, but don't try to replace it. Help users execute their intentions quickly on their mobile phone so that they can go back to the real world quickly. Let's keep a goal to make them look away from the phone 80%-90% of the time

34 unique views, 15 new icons

Final design consisted of 34 unique views, 15 new icons and we transitioned from a browser based web experience to a fully native mobile application.

What UI improvements did we make?

 

Sticky header
  1. Provided a count of Infected and Researcher players in the top-right.
  2. Inspired both a sense of progress for Infected players and a sense of urgency in the Researchers.
  3. Helped make informed decisions

 

Progress Bar and Timer

Don't make players anxious of when next package will arrive. Let them focus on social interactions.

 

Clue Card

Suspicions are color coded and marking suspicion requires fewer interactions

 

How did we adapt good parts to mobile interface?

 

To improve discoverability:
  1. Transitioned the navigation from scroll format to a tab format
  2. Introduced sticky header that includes game-critical information
  3. Introduced drawer menus into the 'rule and role card' to improve accessibility and improve readability
